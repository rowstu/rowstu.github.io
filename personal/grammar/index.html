Grammar Groups

Nouns

A noun is a person, place, thing, or idea. It can be the subject or object of a sentence.

  • Examples: cat, car, happiness, London

Types of Nouns:

  • Common Nouns: general names (e.g., dog, city)
  • Proper Nouns: specific names (e.g., John, Paris)
  • Abstract Nouns: ideas or feelings (e.g., love, freedom)
  • Concrete Nouns: things you can touch or see (e.g., table, mountain)
  • Collective Nouns: groups of things (e.g., team, family)

Pronouns

A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun. It helps avoid repeating the same noun multiple times.

  • Examples: he, she, it, they, we, you

Types of Pronouns:

  • Personal Pronouns: refer to specific people or things (e.g., I, you, he)
  • Possessive Pronouns: show ownership (e.g., mine, yours, hers)
  • Reflexive Pronouns: reflect back to the subject (e.g., myself, yourself, themselves)

Adjectives

An adjective describes or modifies a noun. It gives more detail about a person, place, or thing.

  • Examples: big, tall, beautiful, red

Types of Adjectives:

  • Descriptive Adjectives: describe qualities (e.g., happy, blue)
  • Quantitative Adjectives: describe quantity (e.g., some, several)
  • Demonstrative Adjectives: point to specific things (e.g., this, that)
  • Possessive Adjectives: show ownership (e.g., my, their)

Verbs

A verb is an action word that describes what the subject is doing. It usually follows the noun or pronoun, which is called the subject.

Verb Conjugation

There are 6 different persons in English:

  1. First person singular (I)
  2. Second person singular (You)
  3. Third person singular (He, She, It, One)
  4. First person plural (We)
  5. Second person plural (You)
  6. Third person plural (They)

Verbs change depending on the person and the tense (when the action happens).

Tenses:

  • Simple Present: live, work
  • Simple Past: lived, worked
  • Simple Future: will live, will work

Irregular Verbs:

Irregular verbs do not follow the usual rules for verb forms, like adding “-ed” for the past tense.

Some common irregular verbs are:

  • say
  • get
  • go
  • know
  • think
  • see
  • make
  • come
  • take

Adverbs

An adverb describes or modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb. It often tells how, when, where, or to what extent something happens.

  • Examples: quickly, very, often, here

Types of Adverbs:

  • Manner: how something is done (e.g., quietly, carefully)
  • Time: when something happens (e.g., now, yesterday)
  • Place: where something happens (e.g., here, everywhere)
  • Frequency: how often something happens (e.g., always, never)

Prepositions

A preposition shows the relationship between a noun (or pronoun) and another word in the sentence. It often indicates direction, location, or time.

  • Examples: in, on, under, between, at, over

Conjunctions

A conjunction connects words, phrases, or clauses in a sentence.

  • Examples: and, but, or, because, although

Types of Conjunctions:

  • Coordinating Conjunctions: connect equal parts (e.g., and, but, or)
  • Subordinating Conjunctions: introduce dependent clauses (e.g., because, although)
  • Correlative Conjunctions: work in pairs (e.g., either…or, neither…nor)

Interjections

An interjection is a word or phrase that expresses a strong emotion or reaction.

  • Examples: oh, wow, ouch, hey
